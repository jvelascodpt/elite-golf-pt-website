Golf and Lower Back Pain: Finding the Root Cause

Dr. Jeremy Velasco, PT, DPT

Owner, Elite Golf PT  ·  June 22, 2026  ·  6 min read

If your lower back aches after a round, you're in the majority. Lower back pain is the single most common complaint among golfers, affecting recreational players and Tour pros alike. But here's what most people miss: the spot that hurts is rarely the real problem. Lasting relief starts with finding out why it's happening.

Below, we'll explain why the golf swing is so hard on the lower back, why generic stretches so often fall short, and how a proper physical therapy evaluation pinpoints the true root cause, so you can stop chasing symptoms and actually fix it.

Why the Golf Swing Hurts Your Back

The golf swing asks your spine to do something it isn't built to do over and over: rotate fast, bend sideways, and extend, all at once, hundreds of times per round. A few specific factors make the lower back the weak link:

  • 01 Repetitive rotation. The lumbar spine has very little rotational range to give. When your hips and mid-back (thoracic spine) don't rotate enough, the lower back is forced to make up the difference, every single swing.
  • 02 The "crunch factor." At impact and into the follow-through, the modern swing combines side-bending and extension under high speed. That combination loads the small joints of the lower spine more than almost any other athletic movement.
  • 03 Restrictions up and down the chain. Stiff hips, stiff ankles, or a stiff thoracic spine all leak stress into the lower back. This is why your back is so often the victim, not the cause.
  • 04 Deconditioning. A core and hip complex that can't stabilize at speed lets the spine absorb force it was never meant to handle.

Your back pain is usually a symptom. The real problem is often a stiff hip or mid-back forcing the spine to do a job it was never designed for.

Why Generic Advice Falls Short

Search "golf back pain" and you'll find the same list of stretches everywhere. Sometimes they help for a day or two. But if a restricted hip is the reason your spine is overworking, stretching your back doesn't touch the cause, and the pain keeps coming back the moment you tee it up again.

A swing fault and the pain behind it can have completely different sources from one golfer to the next. Loss of rotation might trace back to your hips for one player and your mid-back for another. Distance loss might start at your ankles, not your shoulders. Without an assessment, there's no way to know which link in your chain is actually failing, and no way to build a plan that works.

How a Golf PT Evaluation Finds the Root Cause

A real evaluation looks at your whole body and your swing together, not just the spot that hurts. Here's what that involves at Elite Golf PT:

1

A Full Movement Screen

Using the TPI Medical Level 3 screen, we measure your body's Body-Swing Connection: rotation, hinge, tilt, stability, mobility, and power. Each is scored, so we can see exactly what your body can and cannot do, the same framework used to assess Tour players.

2

A Kinetic Chain Assessment

Through Gray Institute Applied Functional Science, we look at how your body really moves, in all three planes, as one connected system. This shows us why a problem is happening and how a restriction in your ankle, hip, or mid-back forces your lower back to compensate.

3

Connecting It to Your Swing

Findings only matter if they explain your golf. We tie each physical limitation back to the specific swing fault and the pain it's creating, so you understand the direct line from your body to the ball.

4

Hands-On Examination

As doctors of physical therapy, we assess joint mobility, tissue quality, and strength directly, confirming the root cause rather than guessing at it from a generic chart.

What You'll Walk Away With

01

A Real Diagnosis

Exactly what your body is doing in your swing, screened and scored, not a guess.

02

The Root Cause

The joint, muscle, or movement pattern actually driving your back pain.

03

A Specific Plan

Targeted work built for your body and your swing, mapped session by session.

When to Get Evaluated

It's worth getting assessed sooner rather than later if any of these sound familiar:

  • Back pain that returns every time you play
  • Pain that radiates down your leg, or numbness or tingling in your legs or feet
  • Pain that wakes you at night or doesn't ease with rest
  • Stretches and rest that help for a day, then the pain comes right back

Note: This article is for general education and isn't a substitute for an individualized medical evaluation. If you have significant or persistent pain, please consult a qualified healthcare provider.
